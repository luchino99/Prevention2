Privacy notice — Uelfy Clinical

Last updated: 2026-05-07 · Version: 1.1 (Sprint 3)

This page describes how Uelfy Clinical (the “Platform”) processes personal data on behalf of healthcare providers (“Controllers”) who use the Platform for cardio-nephro-metabolic risk assessment and longitudinal patient monitoring. It is a technical privacy notice intended for transparency to clinicians and end users.

This document is not legal advice. The Controller remains responsible for the lawfulness of processing under Articles 6, 9, 13 and 14 GDPR and must publish its own privacy notice tailored to its data subjects. Uelfy operates as a Processor under Article 28 GDPR governed by a per-tenant Data Processing Agreement.

Authoritative technical companions to this notice (engineering reference, not part of the legal informativa): Data Protection Impact Assessment scaffold (docs/39-DPIA-CARDIO.md), consent enforcement matrix (docs/41-CONSENT-ENFORCEMENT.md), DSR workflow audit (docs/40-DSR-WORKFLOW-AUDIT.md), retention & deletion policy (docs/14-DELETION-POLICY.md), security controls (docs/20-SECURITY.md).

1. Roles under GDPR

Controller. Each Tenant (clinic, hospital, independent practitioner) determines the purposes and means of processing for the patient data it enters into the Platform.

Processor. Uelfy operates the technical infrastructure on behalf of the Controller under a written DPA (Article 28 §3 GDPR). Sub-processors are listed in §11.

2. Categories of personal data processed

  • Identity / contact: full name, email, professional role of clinicians; pseudonymous patient reference (UUID + tenant-supplied external code), patient first/last name (when entered by the clinician), date of birth or birth year, biological sex.
  • Special-category health data (Article 9): vital measurements, lipid panel, glycaemia, kidney function markers, liver function markers, anthropometrics, lifestyle questionnaire answers, validated risk scores, clinician-entered notes, imaging report references.
  • Authentication telemetry: hashed IP address, truncated user-agent, MFA factor verification timestamp, access timestamps for audit purposes.
  • Operational metadata: tenant identifier, professional-patient link assignments, consent records, audit events, retention policy values.

3. Purposes and lawful bases

Each Tenant configures its own lawful basis. The Platform distinguishes between operations that proceed under a non-consent legal basis (clinical care, audit, security) and operations that require explicit opt-in via the granular consent matrix in backend/src/middleware/consent-gate.ts:

  • Provision of clinical risk assessment and follow-up (no consent gate): Article 6(1)(c) (medical-record legal obligation) and Article 6(1)(b) (contract), with Article 9(2)(h) (provision of healthcare by a regulated professional) for special-category data.
  • Audit and security logging (no consent gate): Article 6(1)(c) (Article 30 records-of-processing obligation + Article 5(2) accountability).
  • Notifications (consent-gated, opt-in): Article 6(1)(a) consent. Patient receives email/SMS only after a positive consent record under consent_type = 'notifications'; revocation stops further dispatch on the next cron pass.
  • AI-assisted clinical commentary (consent-gated, opt-in; planned Sprint 5+): Article 6(1)(a) explicit consent and Article 9(2)(a) for special-category data. Activated only where the DPA includes the AI sub-processor addendum.
  • Sharing with external clinician (consent-gated, opt-in; planned Sprint 5+): Article 6(1)(a) explicit consent and Article 9(2)(a) for special-category data. Used for referral / second-opinion flows.
  • Marketing communications (consent-gated, opt-in): Article 6(1)(a) consent. Off by default.

The five consent types (health_data_processing, ai_processing, notifications, data_sharing_clinician, marketing) are versioned and immutable in consent_records: revocation creates a new row with granted=false rather than mutating history (Article 7(1) accountability). Only the four opt-in types are runtime-enforced; health_data_processing is administrative record-keeping under Article 9(2)(h), not a gate.

4. Data minimisation

Uelfy stores only the lab values, vitals and lifestyle answers required to compute the validated scores listed in §10. The Platform does not track marketing analytics, behavioural events outside the audit trail, third-party advertising identifiers, or session recordings. Free-text fields are limited to clinician notes scoped to a single patient record.

5. Retention

Default retention windows can be tightened or extended by each Tenant in their Tenant Settings page (subject to the DPA):

  • Active patient records: kept while the patient is clinically active; soft-deleted on revoke or discharge.
  • Soft-deleted patients: 30-day grace period (configurable), then irreversible anonymisation by the daily cron.
  • Audit events — security category (action LIKE 'auth.%': login, logout, failed_login): 180 days. Aligned with NIS2 Annex II §4 incident-detection benchmark and ISO 27001 A.8.15 logging guidance. Tenant override via retention_days_audit can only tighten this (never widens past 180).
  • Audit events — default category (clinical writes, consent transitions, DSR fulfilment, admin actions, system events): 10 years (configurable 30 days … 10 years). Aligned with Italian medical-deontological code (cartella clinica ≥10 years) and GDPR Article 30 records-of-processing.
  • Resolved alerts: 1 year (configurable 7 days … 5 years).
  • Notification queue: 90 days (configurable 7 days … 1 year).
  • Clinical PDF report storage: 2 years (configurable 90 days … 10 years; database row metadata is kept for traceability after the storage object expires).
  • DSR records: 10 years (Article 12(3) record-keeping).

Implementation: fn_retention_prune() runs daily at 03:00 UTC; fn_anonymize_patient() runs daily at 04:00 UTC. Both are idempotent. Per-category split lives in migration 018; full policy table with legal basis in docs/14-DELETION-POLICY.md §1.1.

6. Data subject rights

Patients exercise their rights through the Controller. The Platform supports the Controller in fulfilling them via the DSR API at /api/v1/admin/dsr with state machine and 30-day Article 12(3) SLA tracking. Detailed gap analysis per Article in docs/40-DSR-WORKFLOW-AUDIT.md:

  • Article 15 (access): API endpoint and state machine implemented; PDF export endpoint exists; the structured machine-readable export (FHIR JSON) is being wired in Sprint 3 task 3.6 — until then, access requests are fulfilled by the PDF export plus the consent and audit JSON included in /api/v1/patients/[id]/export.
  • Article 16 (rectification): in-app edit of patient demographics and clinical context via the standard PUT /api/v1/patients/[id] endpoint, with audit logging of every modification.
  • Article 17 (erasure): DSR kind=erasure triggers fn_anonymize_patient() after a 30-day soft-delete grace, irreversibly stripping all PII while preserving deterministic anonymised score outputs for clinical lineage (legal basis Article 9(2)(j) + Article 89(1) safeguards).
  • Article 18 (restriction): programmatic restriction flag is planned for Sprint 4. Today, restriction is fulfilled operationally via the patient inactivation flag plus a manual processing-restriction note in the patient record.
  • Article 20 (portability): same surface as Article 15 — Sprint 3 task 3.6 ships the FHIR JSON envelope so the patient can transfer the export to another controller without bespoke parsing.
  • Article 21 (objection): programmatic objection enum + UI is planned for Sprint 4. Today, objection is handled operationally via DPO consultation and a manual rejection / restriction decision recorded in the DSR row.
  • Article 7(3) (withdrawal of consent): consent revocation creates a new immutable consent record with granted=false, audited via strict-mode. The next cron pass of any consent-gated worker (notifications, AI commentary, sharing) refuses subsequent operations on that purpose.
  • Article 77 (lodge a complaint): the data subject may lodge a complaint with their national supervisory authority (in Italy: Garante per la Protezione dei Dati Personali — www.garanteprivacy.it).

7. Security of processing (Article 32)

  • Encryption in transit (TLS 1.3) on every endpoint.
  • Encryption at rest (Supabase platform-managed AES-256; per-tenant KMS envelope on roadmap).
  • Mandatory multi-factor authentication for administrative roles; per-role flags for clinician / staff cohorts.
  • Row-level security with FORCE on every PHI table.
  • Strict audit guarantees on consent, deletion, export, report generation, and DSR transitions.
  • Distributed rate limiting (Upstash Redis) to resist abuse.
  • Content Security Policy with no inline scripts and no third-party CDNs in the clinical UI.
  • Annual restore drill SOP; PITR with ≤ 5 min RPO.

8. Pseudonymisation

Patient identifiers in URL paths and audit logs are UUIDs, never names or emails. The patient pseudonymous reference is the Tenant-supplied external code where present, otherwise the synthetic UUID. Audit IP addresses are SHA-256 truncated to 32 characters before persistence.

9. Cross-border data transfers

Default and current state: ZERO transfers outside the EU/EEA. All Platform data is stored and processed in the European Union: Vercel EU region (Frankfurt), Supabase EU region (Central / West), Upstash EU region (West).

Non-EU sub-processors (e.g. OpenAI for the optional AI commentary feature, planned Sprint 5+) will be activated only with the Controller's explicit opt-in via DPA addendum, with Standard Contractual Clauses (Implementing Decision (EU) 2021/914) and a Transfer Impact Assessment on file. Until that addendum is signed and the AI commentary feature is shipped, no patient data leaves the EU under any code path.

10. Children's data

The Platform's clinical scores (SCORE2, SCORE2-Diabetes, ADA, FIB-4, eGFR CKD-EPI 2021, ATP III metabolic syndrome, FRAIL, FLI, BMI, PREDIMED, Mifflin-St Jeor) are validated for adults aged 18 and over. The intended use excludes paediatric populations.

11. Sub-processors

The complete current list of sub-processors used by the Platform under Article 28(2-4) GDPR. Each sub-processor is bound by a written DPA with Uelfy. Controllers are notified in advance of any change to this list per the controller-side DPA.

  • Vercel Inc. — application hosting and edge network (region: EU Frankfurt). DPA + Standard Contractual Clauses on file. In the data path for every request.
  • Supabase (Powerbase Inc.) — Postgres database, authentication (GoTrue), object storage (region: EU Central / EU West). DPA + SCCs on file. Holds all PHI at rest.
  • Upstash Inc. — distributed Redis rate-limit cache (region: EU West Frankfurt). DPA + SCCs on file. Holds ephemeral request counters keyed by (route, identifier hash); no PHI persisted.
  • GitHub Inc. — source code repository and CI workflow execution (US-based). NOT in the data path — no PHI ever reaches GitHub. Holds source code + the committed SBOM + the audit trail of code changes.
  • OpenAI L.L.C. — optional AI commentary (US; OFF by default; planned Sprint 5+; opt-in only via DPA addendum + Article 49 / SCC + TIA). NOT currently in the data path.
